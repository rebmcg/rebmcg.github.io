About

Throughout my scientific career, I have been interested in how cells use combinations of biological and physical mechanisms to regulate cellular functions.

I am currently a postdoc in the joint lab of Dr. Daniel Starr and Dr. G.W. Gant Luxton at UC Davis. My goal is to combine both of my interests in biochemistry and quantitative cell biology to understand how the assembly of the LINC complex is regulated, and how this affects macromolecular crowding and the mechanical properties of the cell.

For my graduate studies, I joined the lab of Dr. Wallace Marshall at UCSF to study the giant ciliate, Stentor coeruleus. Stentor’s ability to regenerate after injury raises many interesting questions about the genetic and physical processes that drive changes in cellular geometry. During regeneration, Stentor’s nucleus changes shape in an extreme and developmentally regulated way, making it a powerful new model system to address this fundamental question in cell biology. Working with a non-model organism meant that I had a limited set of experimental tools available to me, so I had to be creative and resourceful.

As an undergraduate, I joined the lab of Dr. Margot Quinlan at UCLA. I worked entirely with purified proteins. The overall question I was addressing had to do with how the cell sets up the actin cytoskeleton to transmit force, and how mechanical forces act on macromolecules. This project was my introduction into thinking about cell biology and mechanics.

Work

Identified the first molecular driver of the macronuclear shape change cycle in Stentor coeruleus.
During my graduate work in the Marshall Lab at UCSF, I studied Stentor coeruleus, a non-model organism that undergoes a dramatic and developmentally regulated nuclear shape change. During regeneration of Stentor’s oral apparatus, the macronucleus rapidly changes shape: it initially resembles a string of beads, then coalesces into a sphere, and then elongates back into its original moniliform shape. This process provides an excellent opportunity to study how cells regulate the size and shape of their nuclei – however, the molecular drivers of this process were completely unknown. I found that the nuclear transport factor, CSE1, plays a role in altering the dynamics of this shape change: CSE1 is needed for the average volume per node to rapidly increase during condensation, and it is needed for the rapid change in the median number of nodes per macronucleus. CSE1 is also necessary for Stentor to maintain the moniliform shape of its macronucleus after regeneration is completed. Our model is that the transport of proteins into the nucleus alters the volume of the macronucleus, which in turn, alters the shape of the macronucleus due to constraints on the surface area of the macronuclear envelope.
This work is available in a fully open-access journal:
McGillivary, R.M., Sood, P., Hammar, K., and Marshall, W.F. (2023). The nuclear transport factor CSE1 drives macronuclear volume increase and macronuclear node coalescence in Stentor coeruleus. iScience, 107318.
